Finding Oliver

The case of a missing parrot in East Texas is one strange bird.

Joe Cotten knows his story’s details can be distracting. “I start to tell people that my parrot was stolen,” he said. “And they’re, like, ‘Wait, you have a parrot?’ “

Tara and Joe Cotten have a dog, two cats, and an 85-year-old Amazonian parrot that Tara inherited from her grandfather.

Besides, the questions tend to stray from the whole point: how a seemingly straight-forward case of bird abduction ended with the Tyler police charging Joe himself with two crimes; the dramatic three-day trial ending with a made-for-TV speech by the jury foreman; allegations of police misconduct; hints of a jury payoff; and, finally, a sweeping lawsuit against the City of Tyler.

Still, the Cottens get the fascination. So, yes — Joe, 30, and Tara, 26, have an Amazonian parrot. Her name is Oliver, she is bright green and knows only two words, "Oliver" and "help," — which, in combination, have required the Cottens to explain to more than one concerned neighbor that they don’t have any children locked in the house.

Oliver also can yodel an operatic two-note phrase that sounds as if she is unveiling something dramatic, and she’ll sway to music, but only when she feels like it, which isn’t often. "She is very moody," Tara said.

The truth is that owning Oliver is very much like caring for a demanding elderly relative. If ignored for too long, she screams for attention. She grabs food off Joe and Tara’s plates. If she weren’t family, she could easily be considered unpleasant.

But at age 85 Oliver is a living heirloom. Acquired by Tara’s grandfather, who’d opened a pet shop after his service in the Korean War (and who had named her Oliver before finding her sitting on an egg), she was inherited by her father when Tara was 4 years old. Oliver was passed on to Tara three years ago when her father died.

Oliver was stolen in 2013, setting off a series of bizarre events that eventually led to Joe being put on trial and a civil lawsuit filed against the city of Tyler.

Joe and Tara cuddle her while they watch TV (wearing an oven mitt just in case). Prior to her abduction, she would hang out with them in their backyard on a tree branch, from where she would glide to the ground but never leave. Oliver’s wings are curved and graceful and expressive, as much a demonstration of her personality as a part of her physique.

At least they were, until the parrot thieves chopped them off with what appear to be garden shears. It was the first thing that Joe noticed when he arrived at the Tyler police station May 5, 2013. He was exhausted from a sleepless night of worrying, and it made him extremely mad.

The parrot goes missing

Oliver was stolen from a tree in the Cottens' backyard after Tara forgot her outside when she left for work. Joe returned home an hour later and noticed Oliver was missing.

Oliver was swiped on a Sunday. Joe had gone to meet a classmate at the local university, where he takes courses. Tara had brought her outside for some fresh air and to give her a bath. It was the very ordinariness of the situation that caused Tara to forget Oliver was still in her tree when she left for work in nearby Lindale.

Joe immediately noted the house’s strange silence when he returned an hour later, however, and called Tara. “He was panicking,” she said.

Despite his bodybuilder appearance, Joe’s moods can be brittle. He’d met Tara near her home in southern California, when he was a new enlistee in the Marine Corps.

“I wanted to see Iraq for myself,” he said. “I felt like there was more to the war, and I thought the Marine Corps infantry would put me on the front lines.” It did, and today Joe is rated as 60 percent disabled by the Veterans Administration, partially deaf with a titanium shoulder and traumatic brain injury from roadside bombs.

After asking lawn crew workers who had been at the Cottens' house that day if they'd seen Oliver, Tara had a gut feeling the crew had taken the bird.

After talking to Joe, Tara quickly called their lawn service company, the only other people who’d been at the property over the last day. “I was frantic and crying,” Tara said. “I explained the meaning of Oliver. They said if they saw her when they returned on Monday, they’d put her back in her tree.”

The workers’ story was unconvincing, though, and Tara soon concluded Oliver had been abducted by the lawn crew. “I just had a gut intuition,” she said. So when one of the workers came to her door later that afternoon to get paid, she bluffed.

The worker again denied any knowledge of Oliver’s whereabouts; however, two hours later the Cottens’ phone rang. “Do you know anything in regards to a parrot?” the police officer asked.

The victim becomes a criminal

The municipal court building in a strip mall in Tyler where Joe Cotten's trial took place.

Joe and Tara sped from their home southwest of town into Tyler, arriving at the police station just before 5 p.m. Oliver was huddled in the corner of a small interview room off the reception area. Police said a woman had dropped the bird off at the station that afternoon.

It was as they edged their way into the room, crowded with several curious police department employees, that Joe noticed Oliver’s chopped wings. He grabbed his head in his hands. “Oh, my God, they cut the f***ing wings,” he yelled.

Feeling claustrophobic, Joe said he rushed out of the room for some fresh air: “I just stepped my way around the women and out the door.” Tara sobbed. Oliver screeched.

One of the service officers — non-commissioned staff who greet the public — Michelle Lira, called a police officer for assistance. “They almost pushed me on their way out of the interview room," Lira can be heard saying on a tape of the phone call.

Though victims of a parrot theft, Joe and Tara say the Tyler Police Department’s staff had seemed suspicious of them from the moment they arrived at the downtown station. “A barrage of questions came at us about why the bird was outside, why the bird was dehydrated, why did we not take good care of our animals,” Joe recalled.

Judge Allen Ross' courtroom in the municipal court building in Tyler where Joe Cotten's trial took place.

“Attacked,” Tara added. “That’s how I felt.” (Police department spokesman Don Martin said he could not comment on the incident because of pending litigation.)

By the time the police had talked amongst themselves, their account of Joe’s outburst had changed in a small but significant way. Joe“shoved me with his body,” Lira asserted in her report of the incident. The department also concluded that Joe’s use of the F-word in her presence had been criminally offensive. Based on Lira’s complaints, Joe received citations for assaulting an officer and disorderly conduct for the swearing.

“When I entered into that police station, I was so relieved and so happy to get my bird back,” Tara added. “And we left even more of a mess than when we went in.”

The jury moves quickly

Attorney Cody Skipper, seen in his office in Dallas, took on Joe Cotten's case pro bono. What he thought would be a simple ticket dismissal turned into a three-day trial and eventually a civil lawsuit.

Even when compared to other famously tough-on-crime jurisdictions across the state, Smith County and Tyler have forged reputations for unforgiving justice. In 2000, a local man was famously sentenced to 16 years in prison for stealing a Snickers bar.

“It’s over the top, really,” said Scott Henson, a criminal justice-reform advocate and newly named director of The Innocence Project, and who grew up in Tyler. “It was a point of pride almost.”

The police appeared uninterested in settling the charges against Joe.“The only offer made was a dismissalif Joe admitted complete guilt in the assault and disorderly via letter to Michelle,” said his attorney, Cody Skipper.

Though he is a native of nearby Kilgore, Skipper had left for a decade, working as a federal racketeering prosecutor, locking up, among others, members of the Aryan Brotherhood. He’d also lived for two years in Venezuela, where his wife worked for the foreign service, before returning to Texas. Seeing the world had given him some perspective on his home.

Trials in municipal court, where citations are adjudicated, typically last a few hours at most. City of Tyler v. Joe Cotten dragged on for three days. Witnesses were examined, cross-examined and re-examined.

By the second day of the trial, Judge Allen Ross had heard enough to boot the city’s charge against Joe for blurting out the ‘F’ word. At the end of the third day, the jury took 30 minutes to find Joe not guilty on the assault charge.

“You have the distinct pleasure, in my 17, 18 years of doing this, of being the longest jury trial I’ve ever had in this court,” Ross told the five women and one man, adding they were now free to leave.

It was then that the foreman, a gray-haired cosmetologist named Ronnie Riggs, signaled the judge. “We’d like to make a statement,” he said.

The foreman makes a speech

Ronnie Riggs has worked as a cosmetologist in Tyler for more than 40 years. Riggs was the jury foreman in Joe Cotten's trial and read a statement to the courtroom at the end of the trial.

Privately, Riggs had made up his mind on the Cotten case halfway through the first day, when he heard the tape of Lira saying that Joe had “almost” bumped into her. But “We were forced to listen to two more days of them trying to railroad Mr. Cotten.”

Within minutes of closing the jury room door all six members had voted for acquittal.

There was loud agreement the injustice done to Joe had been world-class: If Tyler police would lie about such a small thing, what might they do for more serious crimes? “I told the ladies, ‘We need to make a statement to the court about how ridiculous this was,’ ” Riggs said. “And they asked if I’d do it.”

Though soft-spoken, talking comes easy to Riggs. One day soon after he’d dropped out of high school he’d followed some cute girls in white cosmetology student uniforms to the Barrow Beauty School in downtown Tyler. “I thought, ‘Maybe I ought to be in there,’” he said, and so he enrolled.

Ronnie’s Hair-Um opened for business in 1972 and has not witnessed many changes since. The sign on the front of the building is so old it’s unreadable. Riggs hand-rolls his cigarettes in a back room of the two-room salon and smokes them between cuts. Cuts and colors tend to be leisurely and gossipy; there has never been a TV in the salon because, Riggs notes, “We have too much to talk about.”

Riggs stood for his courtroom speech. “First of all, we’d like to apologize to Mr. Cotten and his wife. We are very embarrassed. We are very upset. We expect a whole lot more than what we got here. This, in our total belief, has been from day one a farce, a waste of this man’s time, and a total waste of the prosecutor’s time.”

He continued: “This is sick. We don’t do this to our citizens.” In fact, Riggs concluded, it “reminds me of Willie Hardy.”

As police chief in the late 1970s, Hardy had orchestrated the largest drug bust in East Texas history based on the testimony of two undercover officers. The officers later confessed to becoming addicted to drugs themselves and falsifying much of their reports, and the cases were thrown out. A few years later, Jennifer Jason Leigh and Jason Patric starred in “Rush,” a movie based on the account of one of the disgraced Tyler cops.

Many of those arrested in the Hardy sweep were Riggs’ customers.“I thought it was going to put me out of business,” he said. But “they all needed a ‘judge haircut’ for their court hearings.” Some remain customers today.

The (alleged) bird thief is charged

In an email to city officials immediately following the verdict, the Tyler court administrator noted the jury’s foul mood. “The jury was very angry,” wrote Cam McCabe. “They felt this was a total waste of time. That it was very obvious that Michelle Lira was lying.”

Normally, Tyler municipal court pays jurors $6 a day for their service. But, McCabe wrote, she decided to pay the jurors on the Cotten case $40 a day. “Maybe that would help calm them down,” she wrote.

It didn’t for Riggs, who has met with the judge and police chief to express his disgust. Skipper, meanwhile, recently filed a sweeping civil lawsuit against Tyler.

“How can a citizen and disabled war veteran of the United States of America be treated with such a despicable disregard of his constitutional rights by police officers, prosecutors, and the City Attorney’s Office?” the complaint reads. Court documents show nearly three dozen potential witnesses for the case.

In April, Blanca Najera-Martinez, a member of the lawn crew, was indicted by a Smith County grand jury, charged with theft for allegedly kidnapping Oliver. “This is the first time in 25-plus years of practicing I’ve been asked to defend someone accused of stealing a bird,” admitted her attorney, Robert Perkins, Jr. He said his client thought Oliver was a stray and had “no intent to permanently deprive the owners” of their parrot.

Joe and Tara don’t take Oliver outside any longer. Her clipped wings have skewed her balance and she occasionally tips over. Recently she went blind in one eye. The Cottens, meanwhile, sold their house and are moving a few miles away.

Skipper has asked himself more than once why he continues to obsess over a case that, at heart, is about one F-bomb, whether a brush was a push and a temporarily missing exotic bird. Even during his closing arguments at trial, he found himself thinking: “I’m having to deliver this ‘Thin Blue Line’ speech in a crappy municipal courtroom” — a reference to the famous documentary film about police corruption.

But he finally decided the incident’s smallness was also the point. If this could happen here, it could happen anywhere; justice is justice, no matter the scale. “The fact that it’s not important,” he said, “is why it’s so important.”
